Italian Culture: Facts, Customs & Traditions

Italian culture traces its roots back to the ancient world and has influenced art, fashion, and food around the world.

Italian culture is the amalgamation of thousands of years of heritage and tradition, tracing its roots back to the Ancient Roman Empire and beyond. It is steeped in the arts, family, architecture, music, and food. Home of the Roman Empire and legendary figures such as Julius Caesar and Nero, Italy was also a major center of the Renaissance and the birthplace of fascism under Benito Mussolini. Culture on the Italian peninsula has flourished for centuries. Below is an overview of Italian customs and traditions as they are known today.

Population of Italy

According to the Italian National Institute of Statistics, Italy is home to approximately 59.6 million individuals as of January 1, 2020. About 96 percent of the population are ethnic Italians, though many other ethnic groups also live in the country, including North African Arabs, Italo-Albanians, Albanians, Germans, Austrians, and other European communities.

Italy’s neighboring countries—France, Switzerland, Austria, and Slovenia—as well as the Mediterranean islands of Sardinia and Sicily, have influenced Italian culture.

Of the total population, 48.7 percent are men and 51.3 percent are women. Children up to age 15 account for 13 percent of the population, 63.8 percent are aged 15–64, and 23.2 percent are 65 or older. Over 14,800 people are aged 100 or more.

The largest share of the population lives in northwest Italy. Rome is the largest city with over 2.8 million residents, while Morterone is the smallest municipality, with around 30 inhabitants.

Languages of Italy

Italian is the official language of the country, and approximately 93 percent of the population speaks it as their native language. Italy is also home to numerous regional dialects, including Sardinian, Friulian, Neapolitan, Sicilian, Ligurian, Piedmontese, Venetian, and Calabrian.

Other languages spoken by native Italians include Albanian, Bavarian, Catalan, Cimbrian, Corsican, Croatian, French, German, Greek, Slovenian, and Walser.

Family Life in Italy

Family is an extremely important value within Italian culture. Family solidarity traditionally focuses on the extended family rather than the nuclear family model common in Western societies.

Italians frequently gather with relatives and enjoy spending time together. Children are often encouraged to remain close to their families into adulthood and to integrate their own families into the broader family network.

Over the past 60 years, the family structure has evolved. The average age of marriage has increased to 31 for women and 34 for men, reflecting greater cohabitation before marriage and a general decline in marriage rates.

Religion in Italy

The predominant religion in Italy is Roman Catholicism, largely due to Vatican City being located in Rome. Roman Catholics and other Christians make up about 80 percent of the population, though only around one-third actively practice.

Italy also has a growing Muslim immigrant community. Muslims, agnostics, and atheists together account for roughly 20 percent of the population.

Attendance at weekly religious services has declined in recent years, dropping from over 18 million attendees in 2006 to approximately 12 million in 2020.

Art and Architecture in Italy

Italy has produced numerous architectural styles, including classical Roman, Renaissance, Baroque, and Neoclassical. It is home to world-famous structures such as the Colosseum and the Leaning Tower of Pisa.

The concept of the basilica originated in Italy, evolving from a Roman public building into a Catholic pilgrimage site. The term derives from Latin and Greek words meaning “royal.”

Italy is also home to many castles, including Fort Bard in Valle d'Aosta, Verrès Castle, and Ussel Castle.

Florence, Venice, and Rome host countless museums, while churches and public buildings also display significant works of art. One of the most notable examples is the Sistine Chapel ceiling, painted by Michelangelo between 1508 and 1512.

Italy has a centuries-long operatic tradition. Opera originated in Italy, and many famous works by composers such as Giuseppe Verdi and Ruggero Leoncavallo are still performed in Italian today. Luciano Pavarotti later helped bring opera to a wider audience.

Italian Food

Italian cuisine has influenced food culture worldwide and is often regarded as an art form. Wine, cheese, and pasta are central to Italian meals, and pasta comes in a wide variety of shapes and styles.

For Italians, food is more than nourishment—it is a central part of life. Family gatherings are frequent and often revolve around shared meals.

Italian food varies greatly by region. Northern Italy favors ingredients such as fish, rice, potatoes, pork, sausages, and cheese, while central Italy is known for dishes like pizza. Southern Italian cuisine features tomatoes, olive oil, garlic, capers, peppers, eggplant, and ricotta cheese.

Wine is also an essential element of Italian culture. Italy is home to some of the world’s most famous vineyards, with evidence of wine production dating back thousands of years.

Italian Fashion

Italy is home to many world-renowned fashion houses, including Armani, Gucci, Benetton, Versace, and Prada. Italians are known for taking dress and personal style seriously.

The late 20th century marked a boom in Italy’s ready-to-wear fashion industry, supported by post-war economic growth. Italian designers have become global leaders in clothing and accessories.

Doing Business in Italy

Italy’s official currency is the euro. Italian business culture is strongly family-oriented, with many small and mid-sized enterprises, as well as larger companies, remaining under family control.

Business meetings in Italy are often less formal than in some other European countries. Personal relationships are highly valued, and Italians tend to prefer verbal communication and established trust.

Italian Holidays

Italians celebrate most Christian holidays. Epiphany on January 6 is marked by the legend of Befana, who delivers gifts to children.

Pasquetta, celebrated on the Monday after Easter, often involves family picnics to welcome spring.

All Saints’ Day on November 1 is observed by decorating the graves of deceased relatives.

Many towns celebrate the feast day of their patron saint, such as San Gennaro in Naples on September 19.

April 25 is Liberation Day, commemorating the end of World War II in Italy in 1945.
